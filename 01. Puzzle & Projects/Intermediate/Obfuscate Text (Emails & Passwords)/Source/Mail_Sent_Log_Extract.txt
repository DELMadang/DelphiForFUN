<P align=left><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif><B><FONT size=2>October 4, 2009:&nbsp; 
</FONT></B><A 
href="C:/Users/Gary Darby/Desktop/Programs/Delphi_Techniques/Number_Words.htm"><FONT 
size=2>Number Words Version 2</FONT></A><FONT size=2>, posted in the Delphi 
Techniques section of DFF, builds on the original version by adding conversion 
back from "number words" to numbers (e.g. seventeen to 17).&nbsp; It also 
solves, or allows you to solve and check, the answer to&nbsp; one of those self 
referencing statement puzzles:&nbsp; <FONT color=#800000>"This sentence holds 
_________ L's, __________ N's, and __________ vowels in all."&nbsp; </FONT>where 
the blank fields are to be filled with number words which make the statement 
true.&nbsp;&nbsp; This is another recent puzzle from my favorite source,&nbsp; 
the&nbsp; Puzzle-A-Day Mensa Calendar.&nbsp;&nbsp;</FONT></FONT></P>
<P align=left><FONT size=2><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif>Note: I have&nbsp; a 
Lazarus/FPC version of this program but I don't feel comfortable in distributing

</FONT><I><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif><FONT color=#800080>As you may or may not

<P align=left><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif><B><FONT size=2>October 16, 
2009:&nbsp; </FONT><A 
href="C:/Users/Gary Darby/Desktop/Programs/Library/bigfloat.htm"><FONT 
size=2>BigFloatTest</FONT></A></B><FONT size=2> was reposted today to 
incorporate two small changes to our large floating point arithmetic unit&nbsp; 
<B>UBigFloatV3</B>.&nbsp; The <B>Add </B>procedure to add one <B>TBigFloat</B> 
number to another could produce erroneous results when a number with a very 
large exponent was added to another with a very large negative exponent (a very 
small number).&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Also procedure <B>Reciprocal</B> could loop and 
produce an "Out of Memory" error under certain conditions.&nbsp; Division uses 
<B>Reciprocal</B> to divide by multiplying and a user encountered the error when 
computing 1/99,999,999,999,999,999,999.&nbsp;&nbsp; Thanks to Charles Doumar for 
the corrections posted yesterday in library file <B>DFFLibV13</B>.&nbsp;&nbsp; 
</FONT></FONT></P>

<P align=left><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif><FONT size=2><B>November 2, 
2009:&nbsp; </B>This card probability question was posted, and partially 
answered, three years ago. </FONT></FONT></P>
<P align=left><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif><FONT size=2><B><FONT 
color=#800000>Given two card rank values, </FONT></B><FONT color=#800000><B>what 
is the probability that there are one or more occurrences of the two values 
being adjacent or with only one intervening card in a well shuffled standard 52 
card deck?</B></FONT></FONT></FONT></P>
<P align=left><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif><FONT size=2>The original version used 
generated random card decks to answer the question experimentally by checking 
lots of decks and counting what proportion of them met the card placement 
conditions.&nbsp; I had asked then about an analytical solution - it took three 
years but programmer and fellow puzzleist Mark Rickert came with the key concept 
(and sample C code) that allows exact solutions.&nbsp; </FONT><A 
href="C:/Users/Gary Darby/Desktop/Programs/Math_Topics/CardProbability.htm"><FONT 
size=2>Card Probability Version 2 </FONT></A><FONT size=2>&nbsp;has my 
implementation of the algorithm and even throws in Mark's C code translated to 
Delphi as a bonus!</FONT></FONT></P>

<P align=left><FONT face=Arial,sans-serif><FONT size=2>Our AARP Bulletin this 
month contains this puzzle, shown here partially solved. It's an example of a 
puzzle game called KenKen; similar to Sudoku except that the board is divided 
into "cages" with an operation and a target value associated with each 
cage.&nbsp; The digits 1 to 5 must be placed on the board so that they can be 
connected with the given operator to form the given target value in every 
cage.&nbsp; In addition, no digit can be repeated in any column or row.&nbsp; My 
</FONT><A href="C:/Users/Gary Darby/Desktop/Programs/KenKen.htm"><FONT 
size=2>KenKen Version 1</FONT></A><FONT size=2> program is a simple playable 
test bed to help&nbsp; investigate algorithms for automatically solving this 
type of puzzle "just for fun".&nbsp;&nbsp; </FONT><SPAN 
style="FONT-SIZE: 12pt"><FONT size=2>At the puzzle owner's request I am 
informing you that KenKen&reg; is a registered trademark of Nextoy, LLC and that 
this site is not affiliated with Nextoy, LLC or KenKen Puzzle 
LLC.<BR></FONT></SPAN></FONT><SPAN

"closed" or "open" routes depending on whether we end up at the city we started

the open route at a specified city but my original version always started at the

<P align=left><FONT size=2 face=Arial>"Instant Insanity" is a variation of an 
older cube arrangement puzzle and one of a large family of similar puzzles. In 
this one, we have four cubes with one of four colors on each face of each 
cube.&nbsp; The objective is to stack the cubes so each column of&nbsp; faces 
has all four colors. </FONT></P>
<P align=left><FONT face=Arial><FONT size=2>A viewer recently asked for help in 
developing a Delphi solver version.&nbsp; Most of the online literature 
describes a graph search algorithm which can be applied to find solutions with

check all 41,472 possible arrangements&nbsp; looking for&nbsp; solutions.&nbsp; 
</FONT><A href="C:/Users/Gary Darby/Desktop/Programs/Instant_Insanity.htm"><FONT
